01 — Electrical Signal from Sensor
Signal, Noise and IIR Filtering
A signal is simply voltage changing over time. Noise is unwanted rapid variation. The IIR filter mathematically attenuates rapid changes while preserving slower trends.

y[n] = α·x[n] + (1−α)·y[n−1]

What happens physically: The potentiometer produces voltage. The ADC reads thousands of times per second. Each reading is one point in the graph. The IIR filter weights the previous value against the new one — high-frequency noise is attenuated automatically. Low α = heavy filtering, slow response. High α = fast response, more noise.

Connection to your IIR filter:
The unit circle is used to visualise where poles and zeros sit in the z-plane. The frequency response of a filter — how much it attenuates each frequency — is computed by evaluating the transfer function H(z) around the unit circle. That is why pole position tells you whether it is a lowpass or highpass filter.

04 — Lowpass vs Highpass — Mirror Images
Two Sides of the Z-Plane
Lowpass and highpass filters are exact mirror images in the z-plane. Lowpass pole sits on the right (near DC). Highpass pole sits on the left (near Nyquist).
